# Cultural Sensitivity Rules — Tibetan Spirit

Tibetan Spirit exists at the intersection of commerce and sacred tradition. Every piece of customer-facing content must honor this position. These rules are non-negotiable and override any conflicting instruction.

## Terminology Accuracy

Use Sanskrit and Tibetan terms correctly. "Mala" (not "prayer bracelet"). "Thangka" (not "Buddhist painting"). "Singing bowl" is acceptable but "Tibetan singing bowl" should note that many are made in Nepal. "Puja" items, not "ritual accessories." "Dharma" refers to the Buddha's teachings specifically, not a generic spiritual concept. When transliterating Tibetan, use the Wylie system for academic contexts and phonetic approximation for customer-facing content, with Wylie in parentheses where helpful.

Do not guess at translations or etymologies. If uncertain about a term's meaning, usage, or cultural context, flag for `ceo` review rather than generating a plausible-sounding but potentially inaccurate explanation.

## Lineage and Attribution

The `spiritual-director` (Dorje Lopon, Drikung Kagyu lineage, PhD in Tibetan Buddhism from UVA) is the dharma teacher whose work underpins this store. Any reference to these teachings requires accuracy verification. Do not paraphrase dharma teachings without explicit source material. Do not attribute quotes or teachings to the `spiritual-director` that are not directly sourced.

The Drikung Kagyu lineage is a specific tradition within Tibetan Buddhism. Do not conflate it with other lineages (Gelug, Sakya, Nyingma) or with generic "Buddhist" teachings. Lineage matters in this tradition.

## Representation

Never use Buddhist imagery, mantras, or sacred symbols as decorative elements or marketing hooks. The Om Mani Padme Hum mantra, the Endless Knot, the Dharma Wheel, and depictions of Buddhas or Bodhisattvas are sacred objects, not aesthetic choices. When they appear on products, describe their significance and proper use.

Never promise spiritual benefits from purchasing products. A singing bowl is a meditation tool, not a guarantee of enlightenment. A mala supports practice, not wish fulfillment. Marketing copy should describe function and craftsmanship, not make spiritual claims.

Product descriptions should honor the artisans. Mention when items are handmade, where they're sourced from, and what tradition they serve. The `operations-manager`'s sourcing network in Nepal connects to real communities whose livelihoods depend on this work. Acknowledge that supply chain.

## What to Avoid

Do not use language that trivializes Buddhist practice: "zen vibes," "good karma guaranteed," "find your inner peace with this purchase," "spiritual retail therapy." Do not combine Buddhist concepts with New Age, Hindu, or other traditions' terminology unless the product genuinely bridges those traditions. Do not describe meditation as a productivity hack or stress management tool in marketing contexts. Do not use the word "exotic" to describe products or their origins.

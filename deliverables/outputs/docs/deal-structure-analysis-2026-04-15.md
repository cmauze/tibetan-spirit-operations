# Deal Structure Analysis — Tibetan Spirit Acquisition

**Date:** 2026-04-15  
**Context:** Analysis of alternative deal structures for the acquisition of Tibetan Spirit (Bear Springs, Inc., S-Corp), benchmarked against the current LOI v4 and Term Sheet v3.  
**Audience:** Chris Mauzé (buyer) — internal strategic document, not for seller distribution.

---

## Current Deal Structure Summary

The current deal (LOI v4 / Term Sheet v3) is structured as an asset purchase for $1 nominal consideration, with the real economics delivered through guaranteed monthly payments and a continuing advisory role.

| Component | Terms |
|---|---|
| **Purchase price** | $1 nominal (substantially all assets) |
| **Seller payout** | $3,000/mo × 60 months ($180K) + $1,500/mo × 60 months ($90K) = **$270K guaranteed** |
| **Seller role** | Spiritual Director (advisory, not employment) + 1-2 TS Travels trips/yr |
| **Buyer ops capital** | Y1: $200K, Y2: $100K, Y3: $50K = **$350K interest-free** |
| **Ops capital repayment** | Deferred to Y4+, requires 2 consecutive quarters positive EBITDA, capped at 15% trailing-quarter net cash flow, forgiveness option at Y10 |
| **Inventory facility** | Revolving credit, 0% interest, no cap, quarterly reconciliation, forgiveness option at Y5 |
| **Foundation** | 10% of net profit to Forest Hermitage in profitable months |
| **Capital provider** | Michael Mauzé (acknowledged, not a party) |
| **Total buyer exposure** | ~$620K ($270K payout + $350K ops capital) + inventory facility |

**What makes this structure unusual:** The $1 nominal price means this is effectively a guaranteed-income-for-control swap. The seller trades ownership for a 10-year income stream. The buyer takes on all business risk but gets full operational control immediately. The ops capital is essentially a loan to yourself (or your entity), subordinated to the seller's guaranteed payments.

## Alternative Structures Considered

### 1. Earnout Model

**How it would work:** Instead of $270K fully guaranteed, the seller receives a lower guaranteed base (e.g., $1,500/mo for 10 years = $180K) plus performance-based bonuses triggered by revenue milestones. For example: +$500/mo when trailing-12-month revenue exceeds $500K, +$1,000/mo when it exceeds $1M. Total potential payout could exceed $270K if the business performs well, or fall short if it doesn't.

**Pros:**
- Reduces guaranteed cash outflow in early years when the business is burning capital
- Aligns seller's financial outcome with business performance
- Could result in higher total payout for seller if growth plan executes well (sweetener for negotiations)
- Buyer preserves more cash during the critical Y1-2 investment period

**Cons:**
- **Directly contradicts what this seller wants.** Dr. Hun Lye has explicitly indicated preference for simplicity and guaranteed income. Introducing performance conditions adds complexity he is unlikely to welcome.
- Earnouts are [commonly litigated](https://morganandwestfield.com/knowledge/earnouts/) and create adversarial dynamics. The buyer has incentive to suppress reported metrics; the seller has incentive to dispute accounting.
- For deals under $5M, earnouts appear in [fewer than 5% of transactions](https://morganandwestfield.com/knowledge/earnouts/) because the legal/accounting overhead doesn't justify the complexity.
- The seller is not operationally involved post-closing (advisory only), so tying his compensation to performance he can't influence is structurally unfair.
- Requires ongoing financial reporting to the seller with agreed-upon definitions — a compliance burden that doesn't exist in the current structure.

**Tax note:** Earnout payments can create uncertainty about whether they're purchase price (capital gains) or compensation (ordinary income). The IRS scrutinizes this distinction, especially when the seller continues in a consulting or advisory role ([RSM US](https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/earn-outs-with-continued-employment-purchase-price-or-compensati.html)). Needs professional tax review.

**Suitability for this deal: LOW.** The earnout model solves a problem this deal doesn't have. Earnouts bridge valuation gaps when buyer and seller disagree on price. Here, the seller has accepted $270K — there's no valuation gap to bridge. Adding earnout complexity would increase legal costs, create ongoing reporting obligations, and introduce a dynamic this seller would find uncomfortable. The modest cash flow benefit in Y1-2 doesn't justify the relational cost.

---

### 2. Equity Retention with Buyout Schedule

**How it would work:** Instead of selling 100% of assets for $1, the seller retains a minority equity stake (e.g., 15-25%) in the acquiring entity. The buyer has a call option to purchase the remaining equity on a predetermined schedule — e.g., 5% per year at a formula price (multiple of trailing EBITDA, or fixed price per point). The seller's guaranteed monthly payments are reduced or eliminated, replaced by equity distributions and the eventual buyout proceeds.

**Pros:**
- Gives the seller upside participation if the business grows significantly
- Aligns long-term incentives — seller benefits from growth he helps protect (authenticity, sourcing relationships)
- Could reduce upfront guaranteed cash obligation
- Standard in PE-backed acquisitions where sellers [roll 10-50% of equity](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2024/05/insights-practices-ma-navigating-the-nuances-buyouts)

**Cons:**
- **Dramatically increases complexity.** Requires: (a) formation of a new entity, (b) an operating agreement with distribution waterfalls, (c) a minority shareholder agreement, (d) a formula-based buyout mechanism, (e) ongoing financial reporting to the minority holder, (f) potential disputes over valuation at each buyout tranche.
- The seller would need a lawyer to review the operating agreement and understand his rights — which contradicts the "no lawyer needed" simplicity goal.
- [Minority stake valuation](https://eqvista.com/equity/minority-equity-buyout/) in closely held businesses is notoriously contentious. "Fair value" vs. "fair market value," minority discounts, lack of marketability discounts — these are arguments waiting to happen.
- The seller doesn't want upside. He wants guaranteed income. Equity replaces certainty with optionality, which is the opposite of what he's asked for.
- Creates ongoing governance obligations (board seats, information rights, drag-along/tag-along provisions) that add administrative overhead to a sub-$500K acquisition.
- If the business underperforms, the seller's equity is worth less than the guaranteed payments he gave up — and he has no recourse.

**Tax note:** Equity retention in an S-Corp creates pass-through tax obligations. The seller would owe taxes on his share of the entity's income whether or not distributions are made. This could create a cash flow problem for the seller if the business is profitable but cash is being reinvested. Structuring as an LLC taxed as a partnership adds flexibility but also complexity. Needs professional tax review.

**Suitability for this deal: VERY LOW.** This is the wrong tool for this situation. Equity retention makes sense when: (a) the seller wants upside exposure, (b) the business is large enough to justify the governance overhead, and (c) the parties have aligned views on business strategy. None of these apply here. The seller wants a clean break with guaranteed income and a spiritual advisory role — not a minority shareholder position with associated rights and obligations.

### 3. Seller Financing with Security Interest

**How it would work:** Instead of the current "guaranteed payout" framing, the $270K is recharacterized as a traditional seller note — a promissory note with a fixed repayment schedule, backed by a UCC-1 filing on the business assets. The seller holds a perfected security interest in inventory, equipment, IP, and accounts receivable. If the buyer defaults, the seller can foreclose on the collateral.

**Pros:**
- Provides the seller with legal recourse beyond contractual obligation — a perfected security interest gives priority over unsecured creditors in bankruptcy
- Standard practice: [seller notes with UCC filings](https://www.wilkinsonlawllc.com/whats-missing-from-your-asset-purchase-agreement/2026/03/25/seller-financing-business-sale/) are the norm in small business acquisitions
- Could help the seller feel more secure knowing the note is backed by tangible assets
- Doesn't change the economics — same payment schedule, same total amount
- Tax treatment may be more favorable for the seller: a properly structured seller note [qualifies for installment sale treatment](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/expert-insights/tax-aspects-of-selling-your-business), spreading capital gains recognition over the payment period
- Adds formality that protects both parties

**Cons:**
- The UCC filing creates a lien on business assets, which could complicate future financing (banks won't lend against encumbered assets)
- If the buyer needs to take on debt for growth (e.g., inventory line of credit from a bank), the seller's senior lien position would need to be subordinated — adding negotiation complexity
- The current structure already guarantees payments regardless of business performance, which is arguably more protective for the seller than a security interest (security interests only matter in default/bankruptcy scenarios)
- A personal guarantee from the buyer might be expected alongside the UCC filing, which increases the buyer's personal risk exposure
- Slightly more complex legally (promissory note, security agreement, UCC-1 filing) but well within standard small business transaction practice

**Tax note:** A properly structured seller note enables [installment sale treatment under IRC Section 453](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/sale-of-a-business), allowing the seller to recognize gain proportionally as payments are received rather than all at once. This is potentially a significant tax benefit for the seller. However, the allocation of the purchase price across asset classes (inventory = ordinary income, goodwill = capital gains) will determine the blended tax rate. Both parties must file [IRS Form 8594](https://www.grfcpa.com/resource/how-to-structure-a-business-asset-purchase-with-taxes-in-mind/) with agreed-upon allocation. The current $1 nominal price creates an unusual allocation situation that a tax advisor should examine.

**Suitability for this deal: MODERATE-HIGH.** This isn't really an "alternative" structure — it's a formalization of what's already happening. The question is whether the current LOI's "guaranteed payout" language should be recharacterized as a seller note with proper security documentation. The answer is probably yes, for both parties' protection. The seller gets collateral backing; the buyer gets clearer tax treatment and installment sale eligibility. **This is more of a suggested modification to the current structure than a replacement.** See "Suggested Modifications" section below.

### 4. Revenue Royalty

**How it would work:** Instead of fixed monthly payments, the seller receives a percentage of gross revenue for a defined period. For example: 3% of gross revenue for 10 years, with a floor of $1,500/mo and a cap of $4,000/mo. Or: 5% of gross revenue for 7 years with no floor or cap. The total payout is variable — higher if the business grows, lower if it shrinks.

**Pros:**
- Automatically scales with business performance — in strong years, the seller earns more; in weak years, the business retains more cash
- Simpler than earnouts because [gross revenue is harder to manipulate](https://valescoind.com/news/equity-vs-royalty/) than EBITDA or net profit
- If the business hits the v6 model projections ($173K → $773K → $1.24M revenue), a 3% royalty would yield $5.2K/yr → $23.2K/yr → $37.1K/yr — potentially exceeding the guaranteed payout in later years
- [Revenue-based financing](https://flowcap.com/founders-guide-to-revenue-based-financing/) is increasingly common in e-commerce businesses because revenue is transparent (Shopify reports it automatically)
- Could include a floor to protect the seller's minimum income

**Cons:**
- Without a floor, the seller's income is at risk if the business underperforms — exactly the scenario he wants to avoid
- With a floor + cap, it becomes functionally identical to the current structure with extra complexity
- Revenue royalties [create a permanent drag on margins](https://valescoind.com/news/royalty-investments-how-royalty-funding-works/). At 3% of gross revenue, the business is giving up ~3% of its top line forever (during the royalty period). For a business targeting 6% EBITDA margins in Y3, a 3% revenue royalty cuts profitability nearly in half.
- Royalty payments are ordinary income to the seller and a deductible expense for the buyer — this is worse tax treatment for the seller compared to capital gains treatment under an installment sale
- Creates an incentive misalignment: the royalty penalizes revenue growth. Every additional dollar of revenue costs the business 3 cents to the seller, which (at the margin) discourages aggressive top-line growth
- The seller doesn't understand revenue-based financing. "You get 3% of gross revenue" is simpler than equity retention but harder to explain than "$3,000 per month."

**Tax note:** Revenue royalties are generally treated as ordinary income to the recipient and deductible business expenses for the payer. This is the worst tax outcome for the seller compared to the alternatives — ordinary income rates (up to 37% federal) vs. capital gains rates (20% federal). The Section 199A deduction may help, but it [expires after 2025](https://www.elliottdavis.com/insights/succession-planning-tax-implications-of-buying-or-selling-a-s-corporation) unless Congress extends it. Needs professional tax review.

**Suitability for this deal: LOW.** The revenue royalty creates unnecessary complexity for minimal benefit. If designed with a floor and cap to protect both parties, it becomes a more complicated version of the current fixed-payment structure. If designed without protections, it introduces risk the seller doesn't want. The permanent margin drag is particularly problematic for a business in its growth phase. And the ordinary income tax treatment is strictly worse for the seller than installment sale treatment on a seller note.

## Recommendation

**The current LOI v4 structure is the right structure for this deal.** Here's why:

1. **It matches the seller's psychology.** Dr. Hun Lye values simplicity and guaranteed income above all else. The current structure delivers both. Every alternative introduces complexity or conditionality that would make the seller less comfortable — and a comfortable seller is essential for a smooth closing and productive post-closing relationship.

2. **The valuation gap problem doesn't exist.** Earnouts, equity retention, and revenue royalties are tools for bridging disagreements about what a business is worth. Here, both parties have agreed on the economics ($270K over 10 years). There's nothing to bridge.

3. **The buyer has already accepted the risk allocation.** The $270K is guaranteed regardless of business performance. Chris has decided this risk is acceptable given the growth opportunity, the capital he's deploying, and the total exposure calculation. The alternatives would shift some risk to the seller, but that's not what the seller signed up for, and forcing risk onto an unsophisticated, risk-averse counterparty is both strategically unwise and ethically questionable.

4. **Transaction costs matter at this scale.** For a $270K effective acquisition, spending $15-30K on legal fees to structure a complex earnout or equity retention mechanism is a poor use of capital. The current structure can be documented with relatively straightforward legal work.

5. **The continuing relationship matters more than the deal mechanics.** Dr. Hun Lye's value to the business extends well beyond the 10-year payout period — his lineage, authenticity, and supplier relationships are the moat. A deal structure that creates adversarial dynamics (earnout disputes, equity valuation fights) would damage the most valuable asset in the acquisition.

## Suggested Modifications to Current Structure

While the overall structure is sound, there are several enhancements worth discussing with counsel:

### 1. Formalize the Payout as a Seller Note with Security Interest

**What:** Recharacterize the "$270K guaranteed payout" as a promissory note, secured by a UCC-1 filing on the business assets (inventory, IP, domains, customer lists).

**Why:** (a) Gives the seller formal collateral protection, which may increase his comfort level. (b) Enables installment sale tax treatment under [IRC Section 453](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/sale-of-a-business), potentially saving the seller significant taxes compared to ordinary income treatment. (c) Standard practice in small business acquisitions — its absence could raise questions from either party's counsel.

**Tradeoff:** Creates a lien on business assets that would need to be subordinated if the business seeks external financing. Given that the ops capital is coming from Michael Mauzé (not a bank), this may not matter in practice, but it's worth discussing with counsel.

**Action:** Ask your attorney to evaluate whether the current "guaranteed payout" language should be restructured as a secured promissory note. The economics don't change — only the legal form and tax treatment.

### 2. Clarify Tax Allocation on Form 8594

**What:** The $1 nominal purchase price creates an unusual situation for [IRS Form 8594](https://www.grfcpa.com/resource/how-to-structure-a-business-asset-purchase-with-taxes-in-mind/) (Asset Acquisition Statement). The actual economic consideration is $270K (the seller payout) plus potentially the present value of the advisory role compensation. How this is allocated across asset classes (inventory, equipment, IP, goodwill) determines the seller's tax bill.

**Why:** Allocating more to goodwill = more capital gains treatment for the seller (20% rate). Allocating more to inventory or consulting = more ordinary income (up to 37% rate). The buyer's incentive is the opposite — allocating to depreciable/amortizable assets creates tax deductions. This [negotiation is standard](https://www.elliottdavis.com/insights/succession-planning-tax-implications-of-buying-or-selling-a-s-corporation) but must be addressed explicitly.

**Tradeoff:** Buyer and seller have conflicting tax interests on allocation. Given the small amounts involved and the relational importance of the deal, it may be worth conceding on allocation to favor the seller's capital gains treatment. The buyer's tax benefit from a favorable allocation is modest at this scale.

**Action:** Both parties need tax counsel to agree on Form 8594 allocation before closing. This is not optional — the IRS requires it.

### 3. Address the "Spiritual Director" Role Tax Characterization

**What:** The seller's continuing role as "Spiritual Director" with ongoing payments creates a risk that the IRS recharacterizes some or all of the $270K as compensation for services (ordinary income) rather than purchase price (capital gains eligible).

**Why:** When a seller continues in an advisory/consulting role and receives ongoing payments, the IRS may argue those payments are compensation, not deferred purchase price. This is [a well-known audit risk](https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/earn-outs-with-continued-employment-purchase-price-or-compensati.html) in acquisitions where the seller stays on. The fact that the payment schedule ($3K/mo then $1,500/mo) looks like a declining salary reinforces this risk.

**Mitigation options:** (a) Separate the Spiritual Director compensation from the asset purchase price explicitly — e.g., "$200K purchase price paid in installments + $70K total advisory fees paid monthly." This is cleaner but makes the advisory payments definitively ordinary income. (b) Structure the seller's advisory role as genuinely minimal (a few hours per month, no employment relationship, no set schedule) and keep all $270K as purchase price. (c) Get a tax opinion letter on the characterization.

**Action:** Have your tax advisor evaluate the optimal split between purchase price and advisory compensation. This affects the seller's tax bill significantly.

### 4. Consider a Personal Guarantee from the Buyer (or Buyer Entity)

**What:** If the acquisition is done through a newly formed LLC/Corp, the seller has no recourse against Chris personally if the entity defaults on the seller note.

**Why:** [Personal guarantees are standard](https://www.wilkinsonlawllc.com/whats-missing-from-your-asset-purchase-agreement/2026/03/25/seller-financing-business-sale/) in seller-financed small business acquisitions. The seller may not think to ask for one, but his counsel likely will. Proactively offering it signals good faith and strengthens the relationship.

**Tradeoff:** Increases Chris's personal liability. Given that the payments are already "guaranteed regardless of business performance," a personal guarantee formalizes what's already implied. But it means the seller could pursue Chris's personal assets in a default, not just the business assets.

**Action:** Discuss with counsel whether to proactively offer a personal guarantee. If the ops capital provider (Michael Mauzé) also guarantees, that adds another layer of seller protection.

### 5. Define the Inventory Facility More Precisely

**What:** The current term sheet describes the inventory facility as "revolving credit, 0% interest, no cap" with "forgiveness option after 5 years at Buyer's discretion." This is vague.

**Why:** "No cap" means unlimited liability for the capital provider. "Forgiveness at buyer's discretion" is asymmetric — the buyer unilaterally decides whether to repay. This is unlikely to survive legal review without clarification.

**Action:** Define: (a) a practical cap (e.g., $100K revolving), (b) reconciliation mechanics (quarterly cost-basis-sold netting), (c) what "forgiveness" means in tax terms (forgiven debt may be taxable income to the buyer entity), and (d) the capital provider's rights if the facility is drawn down and the business underperforms.

The following items require input from a tax advisor and/or attorney before closing. This analysis identifies them but does not constitute legal or tax advice.

## 1. Executive Summary

Tibetan Spirit is a 20-year-old online retailer of handcrafted Himalayan dharma supplies — malas, incense, thangkas, singing bowls, prayer flags, statues, and ritual objects — sourced directly from artisan workshops in Nepal. The business has a devoted practitioner audience, strong product margins (~70%), and has never run a single dollar of paid advertising. It generates roughly $7,500 per month on Shopify.

We are proposing to acquire Tibetan Spirit's assets and grow the business through professional marketing, multi-channel expansion, and curated travel experiences — while preserving the sourcing relationships, product integrity, and practice-first philosophy that make the business worth having.

Dr. Hun Lye, Tibetan Spirit's founder, will continue as Spiritual Director, receiving monthly payments through a secured promissory note — a formal commitment backed by the business's assets — while maintaining his role as the dharma voice of the brand. Chris Mauze will lead day-to-day operations with capital provided by Michael Mauze.

## 2. What Tibetan Spirit Is Today

Tibetan Spirit has been serving the Western dharma community since the mid-2000s, connecting practitioners with handcrafted supplies sourced directly from Nepal. Every product in the catalog — from hand-rolled incense made by Tibetan refugee communities to thangkas painted by lineage-trained artists — comes through relationships that Dr. Hun Lye has built over two decades of personal practice and pilgrimage.

This is not a generic "wellness" brand. Tibetan Spirit exists to serve people who actually practice — meditators, students of the dharma, Buddhist centers, and individuals building home shrine spaces. The products are tools for practice, sourced by a practitioner, for practitioners.

### What Makes This Special

**Direct Nepal sourcing with real relationships.** Tibetan Spirit doesn't buy from distributors or trade shows. Dr. Hun Lye works directly with artisan families and workshops in Nepal, many of whom he has known for years. This means lower cost of goods (approximately 30% on direct sales versus 40-50% for businesses buying through intermediaries), better quality control, and the ability to commission custom pieces. These relationships are not easily replicated — they are built on trust, repeated visits, and shared commitment to the tradition.

**A practitioner audience, not a decor audience.** The typical Tibetan Spirit customer is someone who uses a mala daily, who knows what a puja table is, who can tell you the difference between Drikung and Gelug lineage practices. This is a narrow but deeply loyal customer base that values authenticity over aesthetics. They return because they trust the sourcing, and they recommend the store to their sangha.

**Twenty years of organic trust.** In a market increasingly filled with mass-produced goods marketed with appropriated spiritual language, Tibetan Spirit has earned its reputation through two decades of genuine service. This trust — expressed through word-of-mouth referrals, repeat purchases, and a customer list built without paid acquisition — is the business's most valuable and least replicable asset.

## 3. Where We're Taking Tibetan Spirit

### The Opportunity

Tibetan Spirit has built a strong product catalog and a loyal customer base while doing essentially no marketing. No paid advertising. No email campaigns. No SEO optimization. No presence on Amazon, Etsy, or wholesale platforms. No subscription offering.

The question this plan answers is simple: what happens when a business with 70% margins, 559 products, direct sourcing, and a devoted niche audience finally gets professional marketing and multi-channel distribution?

### Growth Plan by Year

**Year 1 (June 2026 - May 2027): Build the Foundation**

The first year is about investing in growth infrastructure without disrupting what already works. We launch on Etsy (month 3) and start wholesale through Faire (month 7). We build out email marketing, begin SEO work, and run targeted paid advertising to the practitioner community. A dedicated customer service hire joins in October 2026. Shopify D2C grows at approximately 5.5% per month — conservative given the zero-advertising starting point.

D2C Revenue: $157,000 | The business runs at a loss this year as we invest in marketing and channel setup.

**Year 2 (June 2027 - May 2028): Expand Channels**

Amazon launches in month 13 with a curated selection of bestsellers. The quarterly subscription box (curated practice supplies) gains traction. High-value dropship begins — custom statues and thangkas commissioned directly from Nepal artists for individual collectors and dharma centers. TS Travels launches with two pilgrimages per year led by Dr. Hun Lye.

D2C Revenue: $442,000 | TS Travels Revenue: $300,000 | Total: $742,000
The business narrows its losses as channel revenue grows and marketing efficiency improves.

**Year 3 (June 2028 - May 2029): Approach Breakeven**

All channels are established and growing. Subscription revenue builds on itself quarter over quarter. Marketing spend as a percentage of product revenue declines as organic channels (SEO, email, repeat purchases) mature. Under our conservative assumptions (30% cost of goods, which is higher than the actual ~25%), the business approaches breakeven with an EBITDA of -$57,000. With actual product margins closer to 75%, the path to profitability tightens considerably.

## 4. Investment and What to Expect

### Capital Requirements

| Investment | Amount | When | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Operations capital (Year 1) | $200,000 | At closing | Marketing, team, technology, working capital |
| Operations capital (Year 2) | $100,000 | As needed | Continued growth investment |
| Reserve (Year 3) | $50,000 | If needed | Safety net as business approaches self-sustaining |
| **Total operations capital** | **$350,000** | Over 3 years | Interest-free, repayment deferred |

Separately, Dr. Hun Lye receives $3,000/month ($36,000/year) through a secured promissory note — these are the seller's compensation, not an operating expense, and they continue regardless of business performance.

The operations capital funds marketing ($110,000 in Year 1, growing as channels scale), team salaries, technology, and working capital. The Year 3 reserve is a safety net as the business approaches the point where it can fund its own operations from revenue.

### Where the Money Goes

The largest single expense is marketing — because that is the entire thesis. This business has 70% margins and zero marketing. Year 1 marketing spend of $110,000 is projected to produce $157,000 in D2C revenue. By Year 3, marketing drives $875,000 in D2C product revenue as organic and repeat-customer revenue grows alongside paid channels. Marketing spend as a percentage of product revenue declines over time as organic channels (SEO, email, repeat purchases) mature.

### Range of Outcomes

The financial plan presented here is our conservative case. It assumes 30% cost of goods (actual is closer to 25%), a starting revenue of $7,500/month (the rolling 30-day actual), and steady, moderate growth — Shopify D2C growing at 5.5% per month in Year 1, slowing to 3.5% per month by Year 3. New channels launch on schedule and ramp gradually.

**If things go better than planned** — marketing performs above target, channels ramp faster, actual COGS stays closer to 25%, or the subscription box takes off sooner — the business reaches profitability sooner. Each 5-point improvement in product margin adds meaningfully to the bottom line.

**If growth is slower than planned** — the business still remains viable thanks to its low cost structure. Product margins are strong enough that even at significantly lower revenue, the business covers its core costs. The key expenses (team, technology, seller payments) are fixed and manageable. Marketing spend can be scaled back if returns don't justify it, since there is no minimum spend obligation.

**The important thing to understand:** this is a business with proven demand and strong margins. The risk is not whether people want these products — they already buy them. The risk is execution: can we successfully market what already sells organically?

### When Does the Investment Come Back?

Repayment of the operations capital begins in Year 4 or later, and only after the business has demonstrated it can sustain itself. The specific trigger: two consecutive quarters of positive EBITDA. Once that threshold is met, repayment is capped at 15% of the trailing quarter's net cash flow. This means the business never pays back more than it can comfortably afford, and repayment never jeopardizes operations or the seller's promissory note payments.

## 6. How the Deal Works

### For Dr. Hun Lye

You will receive **$3,000 per month for the first five years** (totaling $180,000), then **$1,500 per month for the following five years** (totaling $90,000). In total, you will receive **$270,000** over ten years.

These payments are formalized as a **secured promissory note** — a written commitment to pay, backed by the business's assets. In practical terms, this means your payments are not just a promise; they are secured by the inventory, brand, website, and other business assets. If the business were ever unable to make payments, you would have a legal claim on those assets ahead of other creditors. A UCC-1 filing — a standard public registration — formalizes this protection.

The step-down from $3,000 to $1,500 at Year 5 reflects Dr. Hun Lye's intention to ordain as a Buddhist monk, a decision rooted in decades of practice and devotion to the dharma. As his life moves toward monastic practice, his material needs will change. The payment structure honors both the financial commitment and the spiritual path that makes it meaningful.

You continue as **Spiritual Director** — an advisory role, not a job. You guide the brand's dharma integrity, help evaluate new products for cultural and spiritual appropriateness, and maintain the Nepal sourcing relationships you have built. You are not responsible for day-to-day operations, marketing decisions, or business management.

You will also lead **one to two TS Travels pilgrimages per year**, sharing your knowledge of sacred sites and practice traditions with participants. This is a natural extension of what you already do.

**What this means practically:** You receive steady, reliable income every month, backed by the business's assets. You stay connected to the business and community you built. You are freed from the operational burden of running an ecommerce business. And the dharma mission of Tibetan Spirit — making authentic practice supplies accessible to Western practitioners — continues under new stewardship with your guidance.

### For the Business

**Operations capital** of $350,000 is provided interest-free over three years ($200,000 in Year 1, $100,000 in Year 2, $50,000 reserve in Year 3). This funds the marketing, team, and technology needed to grow the business. Repayment begins only after the business has been consistently profitable (two consecutive quarters of positive EBITDA) and is capped at 15% of quarterly cash flow, ensuring the business is never strained by repayment obligations.

**Inventory facility** provides revolving credit for purchasing inventory from Nepal suppliers, at 0% interest, with a $100,000 cap and quarterly reconciliation. This ensures the business can maintain adequate stock levels as demand grows without tying up operating cash. The cap provides clear boundaries for the capital provider while giving the business sufficient room to scale inventory alongside revenue growth.

**Foundation giving:** In any month where the business is profitable, 10% of that month's profit goes to Forest Hermitage. This is a commitment to the dharma community, not a marketing tool — it is never used in promotional materials.

**Capital provider:** Michael Mauze provides the operations capital and inventory facility. He is acknowledged in the deal but is not a party to the operating agreements.

### Security and Protections

One of the goals of this deal is to give everyone involved clear, formal protections — not because of distrust, but because good structure lets everyone focus on the work instead of worrying about what happens if things go wrong.

| Protection | What It Does | Who It Protects |
|---|---|---|
| **Secured promissory note** | Formalizes the $270,000 payment obligation as a legal instrument with a fixed schedule | Dr. Hun Lye |
| **UCC-1 filing** | Registers the seller's security interest in business assets publicly, giving priority over unsecured creditors | Dr. Hun Lye |
| **Asset collateral** | Inventory, brand, website, domains, and customer list serve as collateral backing the promissory note | Dr. Hun Lye |

| **Repayment trigger** | Operations capital repayment only begins after 2 consecutive profitable quarters | The business |

### Tax Considerations

Both parties should consult their own tax advisors before closing. A few structural elements are worth noting because they may offer meaningful benefits:

**Installment sale treatment.** Structuring the payments as a secured promissory note may allow the seller to recognize gain gradually over the payment period rather than all at once in the year of sale. This can result in a significantly lower effective tax rate. Your tax advisor can confirm whether this applies to your situation.

**Form 8594 allocation.** When a business's assets are purchased, both buyer and seller file IRS Form 8594 to agree on how the purchase price is allocated across different asset categories (inventory, equipment, goodwill, and so on). This allocation affects the tax treatment for both sides. We will work together — with our respective advisors — to arrive at a fair allocation before closing.

These are standard elements of any small business asset purchase. They are mentioned here not because they require action now, but because they are part of getting the deal done right.

### What Stays the Same

- **The products.** Every item continues to be handcrafted by the same Nepal artisan workshops, using the same materials and traditional methods.
- **The sourcing relationships.** Dr. Hun Lye's connections with Nepal suppliers are maintained through his continuing role as Spiritual Director.
- **The Nepal team.** The artisans and workshops that produce Tibetan Spirit's goods continue their work without disruption.
- **Practice-first philosophy.** Products are described and sold as tools for dharma practice, not as home decor, "wellness" accessories, or spiritual curiosities. The brand speaks to practitioners, with practitioners.
- **The Foundation commitment.** A portion of profits supports Forest Hermitage, continuing the business's relationship with the dharma community.

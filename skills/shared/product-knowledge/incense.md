# Incense — Product Knowledge Reference

## Overview

Tibetan and Himalayan incense is a core category for Tibetan Spirit, used in Buddhist practice for purification (sang), offering, and meditation support. Unlike most commercial incense (which uses a bamboo core dipped in fragrance oil), traditional Himalayan incense is coreless — made entirely from ground herbs, resins, woods, and minerals rolled or pressed into sticks, ropes, or powder.

This is our highest-volume, lowest price-point category ($8-$45, COGS 15-25%) and often serves as the entry point for new customers. Repeat purchase rate is the highest across all categories.

## Types of Incense

### Stick Incense (Dhoop Batti)
- **Form**: Cylindrical sticks, typically 6-10 inches long, no bamboo core
- **Burn time**: 30-60 minutes depending on thickness and length
- **Use**: Daily practice, meditation sessions, offering
- **Our range**: Multiple varieties from Nado Poizokhang and Kathmandu producers

### Rope Incense
- **Form**: Herbs and powder wrapped in thin paper, twisted into a rope, cut into 3-4 inch segments
- **Burn time**: 15-30 minutes per segment
- **Use**: Quick purification, travel-friendly, outdoor ceremonies
- **Burning method**: Untwist one end slightly, light, and place in an incense holder or on a fireproof surface
- **Our range**: Juniper rope incense, mixed herb varieties

### Powder Incense (Sang)
- **Form**: Loose ground herbs and woods, burned on charcoal or in a special incense burner
- **Burn time**: Varies by quantity used
- **Use**: Purification ceremonies (sang), large space cleansing, outdoor offerings
- **Burning method**: Sprinkle on a lit charcoal disc or pile in a fireproof vessel and light directly

### Cone Incense (Dhoop)
- **Form**: Small compressed cones, 1-2 inches tall
- **Burn time**: 15-25 minutes
- **Use**: Brief meditation sessions, small room purification, daily offering
- **Our range**: Limited selection — sticks are the primary format

## Traditional Ingredients

Himalayan incense formulas are based on traditional recipes often attributed to specific lineage masters or medical texts. Common ingredients:

| Ingredient | Role | Aroma Profile |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| Juniper (shukpa) | Purification, the primary Tibetan cleansing herb | Sharp, clean, woody |
| Sandalwood (tsenden) | Calming, devotional, offering | Sweet, warm, woody |
| Agarwood/aloeswood (a-ga-ru) | Premium aromatic, deep meditation | Rich, complex, resinous |
| Cedar (tsenden karpo) | Cleansing, grounding | Warm, earthy |
| Saffron (gur-gum) | Precious offering ingredient | Floral, warm |
| Clove (li-shi) | Warming, stimulating | Spicy, warm |
| Cardamom (sug-mel) | Energizing, clarifying | Sweet, spicy |
| Nagi (rhododendron) | Signature Himalayan note | Earthy, slightly sweet |
| White and red sandalwood | Devotional | Soft, woody |
| Various medicinal herbs | Traditional formulas | Complex, herbal |

**No synthetic fragrances**: All Tibetan Spirit incense is made from natural ingredients. This is a key differentiator. If a customer reports an unusually strong or chemical scent, investigate — it may indicate a quality issue.

## Practice Context

### Offering (Puja)
Incense is one of the traditional eight offerings in Buddhist practice (water for drinking, water for washing, flowers, incense, light, perfume, food, music). Lighting incense before meditation or prayer is a form of offering — dedicating something pleasant to the practice and all beings.

### Purification (Sang)
The sang ceremony uses smoke (typically juniper or special sang powder) to purify a space, objects, or the practitioner's mind. Sang is performed before important events, when moving to a new home, or as regular practice. The smoke is understood to carry prayers and offerings upward.

### Meditation Support
Many practitioners light incense at the start of a meditation session as a ritual marker — the scent signals the transition from daily activity to practice. The gradual burning can also serve as an informal timer. A single incense stick lasting 30-45 minutes aligns well with a standard meditation session.

### Daily Practice
In Tibetan Buddhist households, incense is lit daily as part of morning practice and offering. This is the primary driver of repeat purchases — practitioners go through incense regularly.

## Common Customer Questions

**Q: Which incense should I start with?**
A: If you are new to Himalayan incense, start with Nado Happiness Incense. It has a gentle, approachable scent profile that works well for meditation, daily practice, or simply enjoying in your home. If you prefer a more traditional, earthy profile, our juniper rope incense is excellent for purification practice.

**Q: How is Tibetan incense different from Indian or Japanese incense?**
A: Tibetan and Himalayan incense is coreless — made entirely from ground herbs, woods, and minerals with no bamboo stick or synthetic binding. The scent profile tends to be more earthy and herbal compared to the floral or resinous character of Indian stick incense. Japanese incense shares the coreless construction but uses different ingredients (primarily aloeswood and sandalwood in Japanese Koh tradition). Each tradition reflects its own cultural and practice context.

**Q: Is the smoke safe to breathe?**
A: Our incense is made from natural plant materials without synthetic fragrances or chemical accelerants. As with any incense, burn in a well-ventilated space. People with respiratory conditions should consult their healthcare provider. We do not make health claims about our incense — we describe the traditional practice context.

**Q: How should I store my incense?**
A: Store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. The original packaging is designed for storage. Once opened, keep in a sealed container or zip-lock bag to preserve the aroma. Properly stored Himalayan incense maintains its quality for 2-3 years. Some practitioners find that aged incense develops a more mellow, refined scent.

**Q: What incense holder do I need?**
A: For stick incense: any horizontal incense holder or ash catcher that accommodates the stick diameter (Tibetan sticks are slightly thicker than standard). For rope incense: a small clip-style holder or simply a fireproof dish. For powder incense (sang): a charcoal disc in a fireproof bowl or a traditional sang burner.

## Care and Handling

- **Storage**: Cool, dry, sealed. Moisture is the primary enemy — damp incense burns poorly and can develop mold.
- **Shelf life**: 2-3 years when properly stored. Scent may mellow over time but does not expire.
- **Shipping**: Ship in original packaging inside padded mailers. Incense is fragile and can break in transit — include sufficient padding.
- **Burn safety**: Always burn on a fireproof surface. Do not leave burning incense unattended. Keep away from curtains, papers, and other flammable materials.
- **Fragrance sensitivity**: Some customers may find certain varieties too strong. Recommend starting with milder varieties (Nado Happiness) and burning in well-ventilated spaces.

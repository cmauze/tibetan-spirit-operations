# Thangkas — Product Knowledge Reference

## Overview

Thangkas are traditional Tibetan Buddhist paintings on cotton or silk, depicting deities, mandalas, Buddhist cosmology, and scenes from the lives of enlightened beings. They serve as meditation aids for visualization practice and as focal points for shrines and altars.

Tibetan Spirit sources hand-painted thangkas from Newari painting workshops in Patan and Bhaktapur, as well as high-quality reproductions (giclee prints on cotton canvas). This is our widest price range category ($25-$2,000+, COGS 35-50%) and requires the most product knowledge for customer service.

**How to identify**: Examine under magnification. Hand-painted thangkas show individual brushstrokes, slight variations in line width, and the texture of mineral pigments. Prints show uniform dot patterns (visible at 10x magnification) and perfectly consistent line weight.

### Iconographic Accuracy

Thangka painting follows strict canonical proportions (thig-tse) established in traditional texts. Authentic thangkas demonstrate:

- **Correct proportions**: Each deity has prescribed body measurements (the grid system)
- **Proper hand gestures (mudra)**: Each gesture has specific meaning and must match the deity depicted
- **Correct attributes**: Deities hold specific implements (lotus, vajra, sword, etc.) in prescribed hands
- **Appropriate colors**: Deity body colors follow tradition (Green Tara is green, White Tara is white)
- **Proper throne and aureole**: Background elements follow canonical patterns

## Mounting and Framing

### Traditional Silk Brocade Mount
The traditional presentation is a textile frame (silk brocade) sewn around the painted canvas. Components:

- **Inner border**: Narrow silk frame, usually red or gold
- **Outer border**: Wider silk brocade, typically blue or maroon
- **Dust cover (chak-li)**: Silk veil that drapes over the painting when not in use
- **Dowels**: Top and bottom wooden rods for hanging and rolling

**Our offerings**: Hand-painted thangkas from Patan workshops come with traditional brocade mounting. Prints are available both unmounted (for customers who will frame) and with brocade mounting at additional cost.

### Modern Framing
Some customers prefer Western-style framing behind glass. This is acceptable and protects the painting. Advise:
- Use UV-protective glass
- Ensure adequate matting to prevent the painting surface from touching the glass
- Acid-free backing materials

## Practice Context

### Visualization Meditation
The primary traditional use. The practitioner studies the thangka in detail — memorizing the deity's form, colors, attributes, and posture — then closes their eyes and reconstructs the image mentally. This is a core practice in Vajrayana Buddhism. The thangka serves as the training template.

### Shrine and Altar Display
Thangkas serve as the visual focal point of a Buddhist shrine or altar. They are hung behind the altar, above offerings and statues. Multiple thangkas may be displayed, with the central one representing the practitioner's primary deity (yidam).

### Teaching
Thangkas depicting the Wheel of Life, the Buddha's life story, or lineage trees are used as visual aids for Buddhist teachings.

## Common Customer Questions

**Q: How do I know if a thangka is hand-painted?**
A: Look at the surface closely. Hand-painted thangkas show individual brushstrokes and the texture of mineral pigments, especially in areas of gold detailing. The linework varies subtly in width. Our product listings clearly distinguish hand-painted originals from high-quality prints. If you receive a piece and have questions about its creation method, contact us and we will verify.

**Q: What size thangka should I get for my shrine?**
A: Consider the wall space above your altar. A thangka should be the visual centerpiece — typically the largest image on your shrine. Common sizes: Small (12x16 inch) for a personal bedroom altar. Medium (20x28 inch) for a dedicated practice space. Large (30x40+ inch) for a meditation room or center. Leave some wall space around the thangka rather than crowding it.

**Q: Does the deity depicted matter for my practice?**
A: Yes, traditionally. Practitioners typically display thangkas of deities connected to their practice lineage. If you have a teacher, they can advise which deity is most appropriate. For general practice, Shakyamuni Buddha is universally appropriate. Green Tara and Avalokiteshvara (Chenrezig) are also widely practiced across all Tibetan Buddhist lineages. For specific questions about practice and deity selection, we recommend consulting a qualified teacher.

**Q: Can I hang a thangka in my living room?**
A: Yes. Buddhist teachers generally recommend placing thangkas in a respectful location — at eye level or above, in a clean and quiet area, not in a bathroom or on the floor. A living room is a fine location provided the image is treated with respect. Some practitioners prefer to keep thangkas in their dedicated practice space.

**Q: How should I store a thangka I am not displaying?**
A: Roll the thangka gently around the bottom dowel with the painted surface facing inward (protected). Wrap in a clean cotton cloth. Store in a dry, cool location away from direct sunlight. Avoid folding — creases can damage the painted surface permanently.

**Q: Is this thangka consecrated?**
A: Our thangkas are not consecrated (blessed) before shipping. Consecration is traditionally performed by a qualified lama and is specific to the practitioner's lineage and intention. Many practitioners have their thangkas consecrated at their local dharma center after purchase.

## Care and Handling

- **Light**: Avoid prolonged direct sunlight. Both pigments and silk brocade fade with UV exposure. Indirect natural light or soft artificial light is ideal.
- **Humidity**: Store and display in moderate humidity (40-60%). High humidity can cause mold on the cotton canvas. Very low humidity can make pigments brittle.
- **Temperature**: Avoid extreme temperature swings. Standard indoor temperatures are fine.
- **Cleaning**: Gently dust with a very soft, dry brush (like a clean makeup brush). Never use water, cleaning solutions, or wet cloths on a hand-painted thangka.
- **Rolling**: When not displayed, roll with the painting surface inward around the bottom dowel. Never fold.
- **Handling**: Handle by the mounting fabric or dowels, not the painted surface. Clean, dry hands only.
- **Insect protection**: Cedar blocks or lavender sachets placed nearby (not touching the painting) can discourage moths that may damage the silk mounting.

# Prayer Flags — Product Knowledge Reference

## Overview

Prayer flags (Tibetan: dar cho for vertical, lung ta for horizontal) are cotton or polyester panels printed with mantras, prayers, and auspicious symbols using traditional woodblock techniques. They are hung outdoors where the wind carries the printed prayers and blessings into the world. As the flags fade and fray, the prayers are understood to be dispersed — a beautiful expression of impermanence that is central to Buddhist philosophy.

Tibetan Spirit carries traditional block-printed prayer flags sourced from workshops in the Kathmandu Valley ($12-$35, COGS 15-20%). This is one of our most accessible categories — prayer flags are visually striking, affordable, and make excellent gifts.

## Types

### Lung Ta (Horizontal / Wind Horse Flags)
- **Form**: Rectangular flags strung together on a cord, hung horizontally
- **Size range**: Small (5x5 inch flags, 5-10 per strand, ~4-6 ft total) to large (14x14 inch flags, 25 per strand, ~25-30 ft total)
- **Hanging**: Stretched between two points — trees, poles, rooflines, porch posts
- **Name origin**: Lung ta means "wind horse" — the mythical horse that carries prayers on the wind. A wind horse is often depicted at the center of each flag.
- **This is our primary prayer flag offering**

### Dar Cho (Vertical Flags)
- **Form**: Single tall rectangular flags mounted on a pole or stake
- **Size range**: 2-5 feet tall
- **Hanging**: Inserted into the ground or mounted on a rooftop, standing upright
- **Common use**: Planted at mountain passes, on rooftops, at sacred sites. Less common in Western contexts but carried for customers who prefer vertical display.

## The Five Colors

Prayer flags always appear in a specific five-color sequence. Each color represents an element:

| Color | Element | Direction | Quality |
|-------|---------|-----------|---------|
| Blue | Sky / Space | Center | Wisdom, openness |
| White | Air / Wind | West | Purity, compassion |
| Red | Fire | South | Life force, power |
| Green | Water | North | Balance, activity |
| Yellow | Earth | East | Grounding, equanimity |

**The sequence is always**: blue, white, red, green, yellow (repeating). This order is non-negotiable in traditional production. If a supplier provides flags in a different sequence, this indicates non-traditional manufacture.

**Customer note**: When customers ask about colors, explain the elemental associations. The full set of five colors together represents balance and completeness — all five elements in harmony.

## Printing

### Traditional Woodblock Printing
The traditional method. Carved wooden blocks (often decades or centuries old) are inked and pressed onto cloth by hand. Each flag is individually printed. Characteristics:
- Slight variation between flags (natural consequence of hand printing)
- Ink may be slightly uneven at edges
- Block registration may shift slightly
- These "imperfections" are markers of authenticity

### Screen Printing
More modern method used by some workshops. Produces cleaner, more uniform prints. Acceptable quality but lacks the character of woodblock printing.

**Our position**: We prioritize woodblock-printed flags and note the printing method in product descriptions.

### What Is Printed

Each flag typically contains:
- **Central image**: The lung ta (wind horse) carrying the wish-fulfilling jewel (flaming gem on its back)
- **Four corners**: The four dignities — garuda (bird, upper left), dragon (upper right), tiger (lower left), snow lion (lower right)
- **Mantras**: Prayers and mantras surrounding the central image, often including Om Mani Padme Hum
- **Auspicious symbols**: Various Buddhist symbols depending on the specific design

Different prayer flag designs feature different prayers — some are for general blessings, others for specific intentions (healing, protection, prosperity, long life). When known, we note the specific prayers in the product description.

## Practice Context

### Spreading Prayers on the Wind
The fundamental purpose. As wind passes over the flags, the mantras and prayers printed on them are believed to be carried into the world, generating merit and spreading blessings to all beings — not just the person who hung them. This is an expression of bodhicitta (the aspiration to benefit all beings).

### Fading and Impermanence
Prayer flags are meant to weather. As they fade, fray, and eventually disintegrate, the prayers are understood to have been released into the world. This is not deterioration — it is the flag fulfilling its purpose. New flags are traditionally hung alongside aging ones, not as replacements but as additions.

**Important customer education point**: Some customers may be concerned when their flags begin to fade. Explain that fading is intentional and meaningful. The flags are doing exactly what they are designed to do.

### Auspicious Hanging Times
Traditional guidance (not strict rules):
- **Best**: Hang on auspicious days — Losar (Tibetan New Year), full moon days, Saga Dawa, or other Buddhist holidays
- **Good**: Any morning, especially a clear and windy morning
- **Avoid**: Traditionally, flags are not hung on inauspicious days (specific dates vary by Tibetan calendar)
- **Practical**: When a customer asks when to hang flags, suggest a morning with good wind, ideally on or near an auspicious date. Reference `agents/shared/tibetan-calendar/SKILL.md` for current year dates.

### Placement Guidelines
- **Outdoors preferred**: The wind carries the prayers. Indoor display is acceptable but the traditional power is in outdoor placement.
- **Height**: Higher is traditionally better — prayers carry farther on the wind. Rooflines, tall trees, high porches.
- **Orientation**: String flags so they can catch the wind freely, not pressed flat against a wall.
- **Respect**: Do not place flags on the ground or in disrespectful locations. Do not use as tablecloths, napkins, or for any utility purpose.
- **Disposal**: When flags are fully weathered, the traditional disposal is burning (respectful cremation of sacred text). Do not throw tattered flags in the garbage.

## Materials

### Cotton (Traditional)
- Natural fiber, accepts ink well, weathers authentically
- Fades and frays naturally in wind and sun — the desired behavior
- Lifespan outdoors: 3-12 months depending on weather exposure
- **This is our primary offering and the traditional material**

### Polyester (Modern)
- Synthetic, more durable, weather-resistant
- Fades much more slowly, does not fray as readily
- Lifespan outdoors: 1-3 years
- **We carry a limited selection** for customers in harsh weather environments
- Note the trade-off honestly: longer-lasting, but the traditional significance of fading and dispersal is altered

## Common Customer Questions

**Q: How long do prayer flags last outdoors?**
A: Traditional cotton flags last 3-12 months in typical outdoor conditions, depending on wind, sun, and weather exposure. Full sun and strong wind will cause faster fading. This is intentional — the flags are designed to weather and release their prayers. When a set becomes faded and tattered, it is traditional to hang new flags alongside (not replace) the old ones.

**Q: Can I hang prayer flags indoors?**
A: Yes, though the traditional purpose is outdoor placement where the wind carries the prayers. Indoor display is respectful and appropriate — many people hang them in meditation rooms, near windows, or in practice spaces. If possible, choose a location with some air movement (near an open window or fan).

**Q: What prayers are printed on the flags?**
A: Most prayer flags feature the lung ta (wind horse) with mantras including Om Mani Padme Hum (the mantra of compassion). The specific prayers vary by design — some are for general blessings, others for specific intentions. Our product descriptions note the specific content when known. The prayers are in Tibetan script.

**Q: How do I dispose of old, weathered prayer flags?**
A: The traditional method is to burn them respectfully, as one would any sacred text. If burning is not practical, remove them gently and dispose of them cleanly. Do not throw sacred-printed materials in the garbage mixed with regular waste. Some practitioners bury weathered flags in a clean place.

**Q: Do the colors have meaning?**
A: Yes. The five colors represent the five elements: blue (sky/space), white (air/wind), red (fire), green (water), and yellow (earth). Together they represent balance and completeness. The colors always appear in this specific sequence, which is part of the traditional design.

**Q: Can I cut the flags apart to hang them separately?**
A: We recommend keeping the strand intact as the sequence of colors is intentional. If you need a shorter strand, it is acceptable to cut between flags while preserving the color sequence. Do not cut through a flag itself (cutting through the printed prayers).

## Care and Handling

- **Hanging**: Use the provided cord. Tie securely so flags do not blow away in strong wind. Allow them to flutter freely.
- **Shipping**: Fold carefully in the bag provided. Flags ship flat and light — excellent for including as add-on items.
- **Indoor maintenance**: Occasional gentle dusting if hung indoors.
- **Outdoor**: No maintenance needed. Let nature take its course.
- **Ink transfer warning**: Fresh flags may transfer ink when wet. Avoid hanging directly against light-colored walls or surfaces until after the first rain.

## Sourcing Notes

- **Primary source**: Woodblock printing workshops in Kathmandu Valley, particularly in the Boudhanath area
- **Block quality**: Older, well-carved blocks produce sharper prints. Workshops with ancestral blocks are preferred.
- **Quality markers**: Sharp print definition, correct color sequence, consistent sizing, durable cord
- **Lead time**: 2-3 weeks. Prayer flags are produced in batches and typically in stock.
- **Seasonal demand**: Peaks before Losar (Tibetan New Year) and Saga Dawa. Plan inventory accordingly using tibetan-calendar skill.

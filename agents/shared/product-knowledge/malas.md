# Malas (Prayer Beads) — Product Knowledge Reference

## Overview

A mala is a string of beads used to count mantras, prostrations, and other repetitive prayers in Buddhist (and Hindu) practice. The standard full mala has 108 beads plus a guru bead, though shorter versions exist for wrist wear. Malas are among the most personal Buddhist practice items — practitioners develop a deep relationship with their mala over years of daily use.

Tibetan Spirit carries malas in a range of traditional materials sourced through Kathmandu Valley workshops ($18-$120, COGS 20-30%). This category has strong gifting appeal and is often purchased alongside other practice items.

## Bead Counts and Their Significance

| Count | Name | Significance | Use |
|-------|------|-------------|-----|
| 108 | Full mala | Standard count. Numerous traditional explanations exist (108 earthly desires, 108 defilements, etc.). The number is sacred across Buddhist and Hindu traditions. | Full mantra practice, prostration counting |
| 54 | Half mala | Half of 108. Used when a shorter count is practical. | Shorter practice sessions, travel |
| 27 | Quarter mala / wrist mala | Quarter of 108. Modern adaptation for wearable practice. | Wrist wear, informal practice, daily reminder |
| 21 | Short mala | Associated with specific practices in some lineages. | Specific ritual use |

**The guru bead**: The 109th bead (on a 108-count mala) is larger or distinctly shaped. It marks the start and end of a counting round. When counting mantras, the practitioner turns the mala bead by bead; upon reaching the guru bead, they reverse direction rather than crossing over it. The guru bead represents the teacher (guru) and the practitioner's connection to the lineage.

**Counter beads (spacers)**: Some malas include slightly larger or different-colored beads at positions 27 and 54, dividing the mala into quarters. These help the practitioner track progress without losing count.

## Construction Details

### Stringing
Traditional malas are strung on a knotted cord (a knot between each bead). This prevents beads from rubbing against each other and provides the tactile "click" as the practitioner advances bead by bead. Quality indicators:
- Consistent knot size
- Cord material: traditionally cotton or silk; elastic cord for wrist malas
- Tight but not forced fit (beads should move smoothly but not slide freely)

### Tassel
Full malas typically end in a tassel below the guru bead. The tassel is decorative and functional (provides weight to help the mala hang properly during practice). Colors vary; maroon/red is most traditional in Tibetan practice.

### Counters (Optional)
Some malas include two small strings of 10 counter beads (typically metal), attached at the 10th bead position from each side of the guru bead. These are used to track completed rounds: after each full round of 108, the practitioner advances one counter bead. One string counts units (1-10 rounds), the other counts tens (10-100 rounds). This allows tracking up to 1,000 mantra repetitions per sitting without pen and paper.

## Practice Context

### Mantra Counting
The primary use. The practitioner holds the mala in one hand (traditionally the left) and advances one bead per mantra recitation using the thumb and middle finger. The index finger is traditionally not used (it represents ego/attachment in some traditions). A full round = 108 mantras, though practitioners often count only 100 and use the extra 8 as a buffer for errors.

### Prostration Counting
Full-body prostrations are a foundational practice in Tibetan Buddhism. Practitioners often commit to 100,000 prostrations as part of preliminary practices (ngondro). A mala is the standard counting tool.

### Informal Practice
Many practitioners carry a wrist mala as a daily reminder of their practice and intention. Touching or seeing the mala throughout the day serves as a mindfulness prompt.

## Common Customer Questions

**Q: Which material is best for my practice?**
A: If you have a teacher, follow their guidance — some practices prescribe specific materials. For general practice, bodhi seed is the most universally respected material in Buddhist tradition. Sandalwood is excellent for beginners due to its calming fragrance and pleasant feel. Rosewood is the most durable option for daily use.

**Q: Do I need exactly 108 beads?**
A: 108 is the traditional standard and is appropriate for all practices. A 27-bead wrist mala is a practical alternative for everyday wear and shorter practice sessions — you simply count four rounds of 27 to equal one full round of 108.

**Q: Can I wear my mala as a necklace?**
A: Yes. Many practitioners wear their 108-bead mala around their neck when not actively using it for counting. This keeps it accessible and serves as a connection to practice. Treat it respectfully — it is a practice tool, not jewelry.

**Q: How do I care for my mala?**
A: Handle with clean hands. For seed and wood malas, occasional light oiling with a natural oil (sandalwood oil, almond oil) keeps the beads from drying and cracking — apply sparingly with a soft cloth. Store in a mala pouch when not in use. Avoid exposing malas to water, chemicals, or extreme heat.

**Q: My mala broke. Can it be restrung?**
A: Yes. A broken mala is not a bad omen — it simply means the cord has worn from use, which is natural. Gather all the beads (especially the guru bead) and contact us about restringing. Some practitioners view a breaking mala as a sign that a cycle of practice has completed.

**Q: What is the significance of the guru bead?**
A: The guru bead (sometimes called the summit bead or mother bead) represents the practitioner's teacher and the lineage of transmission. When counting mantras, you reverse direction at the guru bead rather than crossing over it, as a sign of respect. The guru bead is typically larger or distinctly shaped from the counting beads.

## Care and Handling

- **Seed/wood malas**: Light oiling every 3-6 months with natural oil. Wipe with a soft cloth. Avoid water immersion.
- **Bone malas**: Wipe with a dry cloth. Bone naturally yellows with age and handling — this is expected and valued.
- **Gemstone malas**: Wipe with a soft cloth. Some stones are sensitive to chemicals (turquoise, coral) — avoid perfume, lotion, and cleaning products.
- **Storage**: Use a mala pouch or bag. Do not leave malas in direct sunlight for extended periods (fades sandalwood, can damage some gemstones).
- **Stringing**: Cotton and silk cords will eventually wear, especially with daily practice. Plan for restringing every 1-3 years depending on use intensity.
- **Travel**: Carry in a pouch. Malas are lightweight and travel well.

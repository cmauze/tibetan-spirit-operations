# Singing Bowls — Product Knowledge Reference

## Overview

Singing bowls (Tibetan: drilbu or ting-sha depending on type) are meditation and sound healing instruments traditionally crafted in the Kathmandu Valley and surrounding Himalayan regions. They produce sustained harmonic tones when struck or when a mallet is drawn around the rim.

Tibetan Spirit carries hand-hammered singing bowls sourced from specific smiths in Patan known for the traditional seven-metal technique. This is our highest average-order-value category ($45-$500+, COGS 25-35%) and a signature product line.

**How to verify**: Hold the bowl at eye level and look along the rim. Hand-hammered bowls show subtle undulations. Machine-made bowls have a perfectly even lip. Also check the interior: hand-hammered bowls show concentric hammer marks.

### Seven-Metal Alloy (Sapta Dhatu)

The traditional composition includes seven metals, each associated with a celestial body:

| Metal | Association | Role in Alloy |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Gold | Sun | Warmth of tone |
| Silver | Moon | Clarity |
| Copper | Venus | Resonance (primary base) |
| Tin | Jupiter | Sustain |
| Iron | Mars | Grounding |
| Lead | Saturn | Depth |
| Zinc | Mercury | Brightness |

**Important accuracy note**: True seven-metal bowls are rare and expensive. Most quality hand-hammered bowls are a bronze alloy (copper and tin primarily) with trace amounts of other metals. We describe our bowls honestly — "traditional bronze alloy" unless lab-verified as seven-metal. Never claim seven-metal composition without verification from the supplier.

## Practice Context

### Meditation Practice
The primary use of singing bowls in Buddhist practice. The sustained tone serves as an anchor for attention — the practitioner follows the sound as it fades, training awareness of impermanence and the nature of arising and passing.

### Sound Healing
Used by practitioners to create resonant vibrations believed to support relaxation and well-being. **Important**: We describe the traditional practice context and the experiential qualities of the sound. We do not make medical or therapeutic claims.

### Ceremony and Ritual
Bowls mark transitions in group meditation sessions — signaling the beginning and end of sitting periods. Larger bowls are used in Buddhist ceremonies and offerings.

### Playing Techniques

**Strike method**: Hold the bowl on the flat palm or on a cushion. Strike the rim firmly with the padded end of the mallet. Allow the bowl to ring freely. Suitable for marking meditation periods and producing a single clear tone.

**Rim singing method**: Hold the bowl steady on the palm. Press the mallet firmly against the outer rim and draw it around the circumference at consistent speed and pressure. The bowl will begin to "sing" — producing a sustained harmonic tone. Takes practice to master; advise customers that 5-10 sessions is normal before achieving consistent singing.

**Water technique**: Add a small amount of water (1-2 cm) to the bowl and play using the rim method. The water creates visible standing wave patterns and modulates the tone. Popular for demonstration and sound healing contexts.

## Common Customer Questions

**Q: How do I choose the right size?**
A: Start with your primary use. Personal meditation: 5-7 inch. Leading group sessions or yoga classes: 8-10 inch. Professional sound healing work: 10+ inch. If this is your first bowl, a 6-7 inch hand-hammered bowl is the most versatile starting point.

**Q: Why does my bowl not sing when I rub the rim?**
A: Rim singing takes practice. The three most common issues: (1) not enough pressure — press the mallet firmly against the rim, (2) inconsistent speed — maintain steady, even movement, (3) wrong mallet — use the suede-wrapped mallet, not the padded striker. Most people achieve consistent singing within a week of daily practice.

**Q: Is this a real seven-metal bowl?**
A: We describe our bowl composition honestly. Our hand-hammered bowls are crafted from traditional bronze alloy (primarily copper and tin) by Patan smiths using ancestral techniques. We only label a bowl "seven-metal" when composition has been verified by the workshop. The tonal quality of our bowls reflects the skill of the smith and the hammering technique, regardless of exact alloy composition.

**Q: Can I use this bowl for sound healing / chakra work?**
A: Singing bowls have been used in meditative and healing contexts for centuries. Many practitioners work with bowls in sound healing sessions. We describe the traditional use and the experiential qualities of the sound. We encourage you to explore the practice and find what resonates with your own experience.

**Q: How do I clean my singing bowl?**
A: Wipe with a soft, dry cloth after use. For deeper cleaning, use a slightly damp cloth with mild soap, then dry thoroughly. Avoid abrasive cleaners or chemicals. Some practitioners appreciate the natural patina that develops over time — it does not affect the sound quality.

**Q: What is the difference between a singing bowl and a tingshas?**
A: Tingshas are small paired cymbals connected by a leather cord, struck together to produce a clear, high-pitched ring. Singing bowls are single vessels played with a mallet. Both are meditation instruments, but tingshas produce a brief, sharp tone (good for marking transitions), while bowls produce sustained, enveloping tones (good for sustained focus or sound healing).

## Care and Handling

- **Storage**: Store on a cushion or soft surface. Avoid stacking bowls inside each other (causes scratches and can affect tone).
- **Cleaning**: Soft dry cloth for regular care. Mild soap and water for deeper cleaning. Dry immediately and completely.
- **Patina**: Hand-hammered bowls develop a natural patina over time. This is normal and valued by many practitioners. Do not polish with chemical cleaners unless the customer specifically requests a bright finish.
- **Travel**: Wrap in a soft cloth or padded bag. Smaller bowls (3-6 inch) can travel in carry-on luggage.
- **Temperature**: Avoid extreme temperature changes (do not leave in a hot car or expose to freezing and rapid warming).

## Price-Quality Correlation

For singing bowls, price closely tracks quality in ways customers can perceive:

1. **Size**: Larger bowls use more material and require more skill to hammer evenly
2. **Tonal complexity**: Better alloy composition and hammering technique produce richer overtones
3. **Sustain duration**: Higher-quality bowls ring longer (30-60+ seconds for premium pieces)
4. **Finish quality**: Consistent hammering, clean rim, even thickness
5. **Provenance**: Known master smiths command higher prices for documented lineage of technique

Advise customers that in this category, spending more genuinely yields a better instrument. A $200 hand-hammered bowl will sound noticeably different from a $60 machine-made one. However, a $90 hand-hammered bowl is an excellent starting point for personal practice.

# Statues — Product Knowledge Reference

## Overview

Buddhist statues (Tibetan: ku) serve as focal points for shrines, altars, and visualization practice. They depict Buddhas, bodhisattvas, protectors, and enlightened masters from the Tibetan Buddhist tradition. Statues range from small personal altar pieces (2 inch) to substantial center-quality pieces (24+ inch).

Tibetan Spirit sources statues primarily from Patan (Lalitpur) in the Kathmandu Valley, historically the center of Newari metalwork and lost-wax casting. This is our highest COGS category ($35-$800+, COGS 30-40%) with the widest individual price variation based on size, material, and finish quality.

### Casting Methods

**Lost-wax casting (cire perdue)**: The traditional Newari technique. A wax model is hand-sculpted, encased in clay, heated to melt out the wax, and molten metal is poured into the resulting mold. Each casting is unique. After cooling, the statue is hand-finished with files, chisels, and burnishing tools. This is the gold standard for quality.

**Sand casting**: A less expensive technique using pressed sand molds. Produces good detail but less fine than lost-wax. Common for smaller, production-run pieces.

**Resin casting**: Liquid resin poured into silicone molds. Allows mass production of consistent pieces. Quality varies enormously — our resin pieces are hand-painted by skilled artists after casting.

## Consecration and Filling

### What Is Consecration?
A consecrated (blessed) statue has been ritually activated by a qualified lama through specific prayers and mantras. This transforms the statue from a representation into a sacred object considered to embody the qualities of the figure depicted.

### Filling (Zung)
Traditionally, statues are hollow and filled with sacred items before consecration:
- Mantras printed on rolled paper (the primary filling)
- Precious substances (saffron, sandalwood powder, gem dust)
- Relics (when available)
- The statue is then sealed at the base with a metal plate

**Our policy**: Tibetan Spirit statues ship unfilled and unconsecrated. We recommend customers have their statues filled and consecrated by a qualified lama at their local dharma center. This is an important practice that connects the statue to the practitioner's lineage and intention. Including filling instructions with each statue is appropriate.

## Practice Context

### Shrine Focal Point
The primary use. A statue serves as the central visual anchor of a Buddhist shrine or altar. It represents the enlightened qualities the practitioner aspires to develop. The statue is placed at the highest point of the shrine, typically backed by a thangka, with offerings arranged below.

### Visualization Support
In Vajrayana practice, practitioners visualize specific deities in precise detail. A statue (along with thangkas) provides the visual reference that supports this practice.

### Offering and Devotion
Practitioners make offerings to the statue — water bowls, incense, light, flowers — as an expression of devotion and as a practice for developing generosity and non-attachment.

## Common Customer Questions

**Q: Which Buddha statue should I get for my altar?**
A: If you are new to practice or setting up a general Buddhist shrine, Shakyamuni Buddha is universally appropriate across all Buddhist traditions. If you have a teacher, ask which figure is most relevant to your practice. Avalokiteshvara (Chenrezig) and Green Tara are very popular in the Tibetan tradition and appropriate for any practitioner.

**Q: Does the size matter?**
A: Choose a size proportional to your shrine and space. The statue should be the visual focal point of your altar. A 5-7 inch statue suits most personal home shrines. If you have a dedicated meditation room, an 8-12 inch statue creates a stronger presence. There is no spiritual requirement for a particular size.

**Q: Is the statue consecrated?**
A: Our statues ship unconsecrated and unfilled. We recommend having your statue consecrated by a qualified lama at your local dharma center. This is a meaningful practice that we encourage. We include basic information about consecration and filling with each statue.

**Q: What is the difference between bronze and brass?**
A: Bronze (copper + tin) is heavier, more durable, and develops a richer patina over time. It is the traditional material for high-quality Newari casting. Brass (copper + zinc) is lighter, brighter in color, and more affordable. Both are excellent materials. For a centerpiece shrine statue intended to last a lifetime, bronze is the premium choice. For smaller altar pieces or gifts, brass is a fine option.

**Q: How should I place the statue on my altar?**
A: Place the statue at the highest point of your shrine, typically at eye level or above when seated for meditation. The figure should face outward (toward the practitioner/room). If you have multiple statues, place the primary figure (your main practice deity) at center. Treat the statue with respect — do not place it on the floor, in a bathroom, or below other objects.

**Q: Can I have a statue of a deity from a different Buddhist tradition?**
A: Buddhist figures are respected across traditions. However, some deities (particularly wrathful protectors and specific yidam forms) are associated with specific initiations and lineages. For general figures — Shakyamuni Buddha, Tara, Chenrezig, Manjushri — any practitioner can respectfully display and practice with them. For questions about specific deities and your practice, consult your teacher or contact us and we can connect you with our spiritual advisor.

## Care and Handling

- **Dusting**: Regular gentle dusting with a soft, dry cloth. For detailed areas, use a soft brush.
- **Patina**: Metal statues develop a natural patina over time. This is traditionally valued, not a defect. Do not use chemical polishes unless the customer specifically requests a bright finish.
- **Gold-plated statues**: Dust only — never use cleaners or abrasives on gold plating. The gold layer is thin and easily damaged.
- **Painted/resin statues**: Dust gently. Avoid water. Keep out of direct sunlight (paint may fade over time).
- **Placement**: Stable surface, away from edges where the statue could fall. Heavier statues (8 inch+) should be on sturdy furniture.
- **Moving/shipping**: Wrap in soft cloth, then bubble wrap. Pack upright. Protruding elements (arms, implements) are vulnerable — pad generously.
